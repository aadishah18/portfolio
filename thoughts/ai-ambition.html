Most Companies Don't Need AI. They Need Ambition.

June 19, 2026  ·  5 min read

One of the strangest things about AI is that everyone already seems to know what they're going to do with the savings.

They're going to cut costs.

Which is funny, because for decades companies have been telling investors the exact opposite story. Every strategy deck, every board presentation, every annual planning session eventually comes back to the same idea: if only we had more resources, we could do more.

More customers.

More markets.

Better products.

Faster execution.

Then AI shows up and suddenly the conversation becomes smaller.

How many people can we remove?

How much can we automate?

How much more efficient can we become?

I've spent the last few months building AI workflows, talking to founders, and watching companies experiment with these tools. The thing that keeps bothering me is how quickly the conversation collapses into labor replacement. It's as if we collectively received a windfall and immediately started discussing what we could cut instead of what we could build.

The more I think about it, the more I keep coming back to an old Goldman Sachs saying:

There are no small deals. Only small fees.

People hear that and assume it's arrogance. It's actually economics.

Goldman never lacked the expertise to advise smaller companies. What it lacked was a cost structure that made those opportunities worth pursuing. A $50 million transaction still required analysts, models, presentations, research, diligence, and coordination. The processing burden was largely fixed. The fee wasn't.

So Goldman moved upstream.

Not because smaller opportunities were uninteresting. Because they were uneconomical.

That's why AI feels different to me.

For the first time, we're directly attacking the processing layer itself.

Not judgment.

Not relationships.

Not trust.

Processing.

The work that sits between intention and execution.

The work that nobody really wants to do but somebody always had to.

A lot of modern organizations are built around this reality. If you zoom out, an absurd amount of white-collar work is just information moving from one place to another. A deck summarizing a report. A report summarizing customer conversations. A CRM update documenting a meeting. A spreadsheet feeding another spreadsheet.

Useful work.

Necessary work.

But still processing.

I've started thinking about organizations as collections of tokens.

Not AI tokens.

Human tokens.

Attention.

Energy.

Judgment.

Trust.

Relationships.

Creativity.

Every company gets a finite number of them.

Historically, a huge percentage of those tokens have been consumed by processing. The economics demanded it. If you wanted analysis, somebody had to do the analysis. If you wanted information organized, somebody had to organize it. If you wanted a report, somebody had to build the report.

The work was valuable because the labor was expensive.

That equation is changing.

At Adaptive, some of the most useful AI systems weren't particularly glamorous. Nobody was trying to build AGI. Nobody was replacing entire teams. We were removing friction. Manual CRM updates. Data cleanup. Administrative workflows. The boring stuff.

What struck me wasn't that the work disappeared.

What struck me was what remained.

Customers still mattered.

Strategy still mattered.

Sales still mattered.

Relationships still mattered.

The valuable work survived.

What disappeared was the tax.

The overhead sitting between a person and the thing they were actually trying to accomplish.

And once you remove the tax, something interesting happens.

The company suddenly has spare capacity.

That's where I think most people get the AI story wrong.

They assume spare capacity naturally translates into cost reduction.

Sometimes it will.

But history suggests something else often happens.

People expand into the constraint.

The internet didn't create smaller businesses.

It created larger ones.

Cloud computing didn't create smaller software companies.

It created larger ones.

Every time a major constraint gets removed, the winners tend to be the people who use the newly available capacity to pursue things that were previously out of reach.

That's why I keep coming back to ambition.

A founder I spoke with recently runs a large fitness business. On the surface, he sells products. In reality, he's selling transformation. The products are just the mechanism.

For years there were things he wanted to offer customers that didn't make economic sense. Personalized coaching. Better accountability. More individualized support. A deeper relationship with customers after the initial purchase.

The vision existed.

The economics didn't.

Now the economics are changing.

I suspect this is going to happen everywhere.

Law firms.

Banks.

Consultancies.

Startups.

Healthcare.

Education.

Not because AI magically solves their problems.

Because AI changes the cost structure underneath them.

And when the cost structure changes, entire categories of opportunity suddenly become viable.

The companies that benefit most from AI won't necessarily be the ones that cut the deepest.

They'll be the ones with the clearest vision.

The ones that already know what they would do if they had more capacity.

The ones that understand the difference between efficiency and ambition.

Everyone talks about AI as a labor story.

I increasingly think it's a strategy story.

A lot of companies are about to receive a windfall.

The question isn't whether they'll save money.

The question is whether they'll spend the surplus on something bigger than themselves.

Because the companies that win won't use AI to become smaller.

They'll use AI to become more ambitious.
