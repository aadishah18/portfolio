The Future of AI Is Surprisingly Blue Collar

June 19, 2026  ·  4 min read

If you had asked someone five years ago what the most important job in AI would be, they probably would have said machine learning engineer.

Five years from now, the answer might be electrician.

That sounds ridiculous until you think about what is actually happening.

The market understands the first-order effect. Models are getting smarter. Agents are getting better. More work is being automated.

The second-order effects are where things get interesting.

Here is what I see from a mechanical engineering perspective.

At USC, I have spent the last few years working on rockets and race cars. One thing engineering teaches you very quickly is that every system eventually becomes constrained by its bottleneck.

You can have the most sophisticated rocket engine in the world. It does not matter if the cooling system fails.

You can have the perfect aerodynamic design. It does not matter if manufacturing cannot produce it reliably.

You can have an elegant simulation. It does not matter if the material cracks under real operating conditions.

The bottleneck always wins.

That is why I have started looking at AI differently.

Most people see software.

I increasingly see infrastructure.

The conversation today revolves around models. OpenAI. Anthropic. Gemini. Which company has the smartest model? Which startup has the best agents? Which application layer captures the most value?

Those are good questions.

I just do not think they are the most important ones anymore.

Because intelligence is becoming abundant.

Infrastructure is not.

Every token generated by an AI model requires electricity.

Every rack full of GPUs generates heat.

Every data center requires cooling.

Every cooling system requires power.

Every megawatt requires transmission infrastructure.

Every transmission project requires years.

You cannot prompt your way around thermodynamics.

That is why some of the most interesting AI-adjacent businesses do not really look like AI businesses at all.

Memory.

Photonics.

Power.

Cooling.

Transmission.

Networking.

The stuff most software investors skip over.

A few months ago I started noticing something strange. Some of the smartest investors I follow were spending less time talking about models and more time talking about the infrastructure underneath them.

Memory manufacturers.

Networking companies.

Power-intensive businesses.

Even Bitcoin miners.

At first that seemed odd.

Then it started making sense.

The reason memory is interesting is not memory.

The reason photonics is interesting is not photonics.

The reason TE Connectivity is interesting is not connectors.

The reason HIVE is interesting is not Bitcoin.

The reason Nebius is interesting is not cloud computing.

They are interesting because they sit directly on the path between intelligence and reality.

And that path is getting crowded.

Take memory.

Everyone talks about GPUs.

Very few people talk about what happens when every major AI company in the world is competing for the same memory supply chain.

HBM demand is exploding.

Supply cannot magically appear overnight.

New fabrication capacity takes years to build.

The bottleneck moves.

And when the bottleneck moves, pricing power moves with it.

The same thing is happening in networking.

The market narrative is still centered on compute. But eventually information has to move between chips, racks, clusters, and data centers. Processing information is only half the problem. Moving it is the other half.

Or power.

This is the one I think people still underestimate.

Most AI forecasts implicitly assume that if demand exists, infrastructure will appear.

Engineers know that is not how the world works.

The world moves at the speed of permits.

At the speed of construction.

At the speed of transformers.

At the speed of transmission lines.

At the speed of reality.

That is why I think AI is starting to look less like a software revolution and more like an industrial one.

Railroads created demand for steel.

The internet created demand for fiber.

Cloud computing created demand for data centers.

AI is creating demand for physical infrastructure at a scale we have not seen in years.

I recently read Leopold's latest piece, and the names that stood out to me were not the obvious ones. It was TE. It was CLSK. It was HIVE. It was the companies sitting on the edge of power, memory, networking, and compute. That caught my attention not because those names are sexy, but because they are exactly the kind of names that become interesting when the bottleneck moves.

That is the real point.

The market is still asking:

Which AI company wins?

I think the more interesting question is:

What becomes scarce when intelligence becomes abundant?

As a mechanical engineering student, that is the question I keep coming back to.

Because every system eventually becomes constrained by its bottleneck.

For years, AI's bottleneck was intelligence.

Now intelligence is becoming abundant.

The bottleneck is moving.

And whenever the bottleneck moves, value tends to move with it.

The biggest winners of the AI era may not be the companies creating intelligence.

They may be the companies making it possible to run.
